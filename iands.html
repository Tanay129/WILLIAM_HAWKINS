"A Passage to India: William Hawkins' Expedition into the Soul of India"

INTRODUCTION

On August 24, 1607, William Keeling and Sir William Hawkins, an ambassador for the English East India Company, set sail for Surat in the ship Hector. However, soon after the Hector landed in Surat in August 1608, Hawkins was taken prisoner by the Portuguese. Hawkins had been despatched to Surat by the English East India Company in order to secure official authorisation for the construction of an English factory. He was told that the Portuguese king controlled all of the ports and that no one could enter India without a permit from him. Hawkins was able to get a pass despite the difficult circumstances to travel to Agra. Subsequently, Hawkins continued his journey to the court of Mughal Emperor Jahangir in Agra. It was a perilous and arduous journey, but on April 16, 1609, he finally reached Agra. During his time in the Mughal court, which lasted for two years, Hawkins served as an envoy. He presented a personal letter from the English East India Company to Emperor Jahangir, which was read aloud in the court.

WILLIAM HAWKINS' CONTRIBUTION TO INDIA

For two years, Hawkins served as an emissary at the court of Jahangir, the Mughal emperor. William Hawkins was despatched by the English East India Company to Surat to request official approval for the construction of an English factory. Captain's letter was read out in court with assistance, and he was then led to a separate conference room for more conversations. Due to the fluency of the language among Sir William Hawkins, Jahangir, and his ministers, the conversation was conducted in Turkish. The maintenance fee was requested by Emperor Jahangir Khan in exchange for the construction of the English East India Company plant. The Emperor Jahangir referred to Captain William Hawkins as the English Khan. He gained notoriety for establishing an English factory in Surat.

STRUGGLES THAT WILLIAM HAWKINS FACED

Sir William Hawkins faced many difficulties and dangers when he got to the port of Surat. With 25,000 gold coins and a private message for the Mughal Emperor Jahangir, Hawkins encountered a string of unfavourable circumstances. His gold was taken by pirates, which resulted in a large loss. Additionally, he was often targeted for murder while he was on land. Despite his persistent attempts over the course of more than two years, Hawkins was forced to return to England empty-handed and without his belongings.

THE BATTLE OF SWALLY

Coincidentally, a fleet of 16 Portuguese ships entered Surat on September 13, 1612. Captain Best made the decision to write to the Emperor on September 22, 1612, requesting permission to engage in commerce and establish a factory in Surat. He intended to leave the country if he was rejected. King James, I may have renewed the company's charter in 1609 on the condition that it would be revoked if no successful endeavors were completed within three years. On September 30, 1612, Captain Best received word that Canning and William, two of his men, had been taken into custody while on the coast. A ship belonging to the Gujarati governor was impounded by Captain Best, who offered to free it in return for his soldiers because he was afraid of the worst. The English East India Company's foothold in India began to grow with this relatively minor naval battle, though its significance wouldn't be felt for many generations. The English East India Company was persuaded by this conflict to build a small navy to protect its commercial interests from pirates and other European nations. This modest beginning is viewed as the foundation of the contemporary Indian Navy. Swally is referred to as Suvali in another language. The modern-day village of Suvali is close to the Suvali port, which is found in Surat, India. The port was built by the English because they believed it to be secure and sheltered from military assaults as well as sudden squalls. Additionally, Swally was navigable during low tides, making it convenient for the English to use the location for their early trade with Surat. India was a short distance away and going there was simple. As the French and Portuguese also operated out of Surat, using the ports there presented several challenges.

THE SILK ROAD

A large commercial route that connected Eurasia and North Africa by land and sea was known as the Silk Road. Chinese silk, a highly prized and expensive item that traders moved over these trade networks, gave rise to the popular name "Silk Road". Trade increased as a result of political stability and technological advancements. Travelers exchanged a wide variety of items, including ideas, animals, spices, and other commodities, as more trade routes opened up. Traders have to devise effective means of transporting their goods. The camel was a preferred means of transportation for transcontinental journeys. From the second millennium BCE forward, nomadic peoples in central Asia began domesticating camels. For instance, the Han Chinese transported war supplies on camels they had captured. Camels were able to carry up to 500 pounds at once and could tolerate the severe desert conditions in Central Asia. Pack animals, particularly camels, made it possible to move goods over land on the Silk Road. The ocean served as a means of transportation for both merchants and sailors. To properly navigate the waters, sailors needed a solid grasp of wind patterns, directions, and storm systems. For instance, in the Indian Ocean, the monsoon winds come from the southwest in the summer and the northeast in the winter. Merchants were able to travel from the Red Sea between Egypt and Arabia to India in the summer and back to the Red Sea in the winter because of a south-western breeze that pushed them east. It was shared among seafarers and spread beyond the Indian Ocean.
